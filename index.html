Journey through the
Solar System

Introduction

Welcome to space!

You've just exited Earth's orbit and now you have a whole universe to explore! But let's keep it simple, first. We should just take a stroll around the neighbourhood, for now! You might know this neighbourhood as the Solar System and you've been assigned to explore it!

NASA has designated you and one other classmate to uncover some more information about the 7 other planets that surround us.

Your task will begin the closest planet to the Sun - Mercury!
So strap in! It's gonna be a long journey! Don't forget to enjoy the view!

Task

Throughout your journey, you'll come across loads of information. You'll have the extremely important task to write down everything you find in the ship's log book, so that NASA can document your findings when you return to Earth.

A great crew should be well organized! We suggest that you decide between yourselves which one of these two roles you will take:

  • Navigator - The crew member responsible for "navigating" the ship and the web for the given resources and discover the information that is being requested by the scientists back on Earth;
  • Data Collector - The crew member responsible for clearly registering the all the found data on each planet;

The scientists back at NASA need you to answer these 5 questions for each planet:

  1. How long is a day?
  2. How long is a year?
  3. How many moons does it have?
  4. What is its maximum and minimum temperature?
  5. What type of planet is it?
IMPORTANT:
The 1st and 2nd questions need to be answered in Earth days.
The 5th question only accepts one of three possible answers: Terrestrial, Gas Giant or Ice Giant.

You should also find 2 interesting facts that the scientists think that makes the planet you're visiting different from the others. Also, if you want to, you research the planet a bit more and find 1 fact that the scientists didn't remember to ask your crew to find.

Finally, after you finish filling your log book in, you'll need to create a poster about one of the planets you visited. Every poster will have to be about a different planet. Your group will be able to choose which planet you'll create the poster about. The order by which each group will choose will take into account:

  • The amount of points collected in the first part (you can read a little bit more about the points in the Evaluation section);
  • The time at which you deliver your log book to your teacher;
In this poster, you'll have to write down the information you wrote on your log book about the planet you're making the poster on, as well as some more information so that you can know the planet a bit more. Ideally, the poster should have the following sections:
  • Basic Planetary Info
  • Three facts that make the planet unique
  • Appearances in pop culture
  • One of the three:
    • Composition of the atmosphere
    • Description of a satellite
    • Satellites or robots that visited it

The information about some of the selectable topics might not be on the links available in the Resources section! You're supposed to search them on your own!

This poster can follow any design you like, but it must fit into an A4 page. You should also include a picture of the planet! After you finish these posters they'll be spread out throughout your school, so don't forget to make them look nice and captivating!

Good luck!

Evaluation

In this WebQuest your group can be awarded a total of 200 points. The first task - answering the questions in the log book - you can get a maximum of 120 points and in the second task - the poster - you can get a maximum of 80 points. Below, there is a more detailed view of the amount of points per task.

Log Book

For the evaluation of your discoveries, as explained at the beginning of the Process section, you should write down the answers on a piece of paper, clearly identifying the name of the section and listing each answer with the number or letter of its respective question, as shown in the log book.

  • The Solar System section is each worth 4 points. Since in this section there are 8 possible answers, each one you get right is worth 0.5 points.
  • The Asteroid Belt section is also worth 4 points, where each question is worth 2 points.
  • The remaining sections are worth 14 points, where each question is worth 2 points.

Poster

The table below represents how the 80 points are distributed. In each of the topics presented, you can be evaluated with:

  • Insufficient, where you won't be rewarded with any points
  • Good, where you will rewarded with half of the maximum points of that topic
